HANGING GARDENS

DESCRIPTION

The Hanging Gardens, in Mumbai, also known as Pherozeshah Mehta Gardens, are terraced gardens perched at the top of Malabar Hill, on its western side, just opposite the Kamala Nehru Park. They provide sunset views over the Arabian Sea and feature numerous hedges carved into the shapes of animals. The park was designed and laid out in 1881 by Ulhas Ghapokar over Bombay's main reservoir, some say to cover the water from the potentially contaminating activity of the nearby Towers of Silence. When seen from the air, the walkway inside the park (Hanging Gardens Path), spell out the letters PMG (Pherozeshah Mehta Gardens) in cursive.

A green, serene space right in the middle of a bustling city, the Hanging Gardens of Mumbai is a terrace garden perched on the western side of the Malabar Hill. With a history of over 138 years, it stands as a popular spot where Mumbaikars come to spend a few moments in the cradle of nature, away from the hustle and bustle of the ever-buzzing city. The park offers outstanding sunset views of the Arabian Sea, making it a favorite evening hangout spot for travelers. Today, the Hanging Gardens serves as a sprawling green space in Mumbai, a city that doesn’t have such places, parks, and gardens in abundance. It is a great spot for joggers and morning walkers to start their day on a fresh note. For the tiny tots, the garden is a lovely place to run and play around. Over the years, this place has also turned into a major tourist attraction and a picnic spot. It is also frequented by shutterbugs who want to capture some stunning shots of the sun setting over the majestic Arabian Sea. More than anything else, the Hanging Gardens is a place where you can be close to nature, surrounded by greenery. HISTORY

The Hanging Gardens, one of the most relaxing places to visit in Mumbai, dates back to 1881. This century-old garden was designed and laid out by Ulhas Ghapokar over the main water reservoir of Mumbai. It is believed that the garden was designed to serve a deeper purpose than just being a beautiful space for relaxation. It was created to protect the city’s drinking water from getting contaminated from the activities taking place at the nearby Tower of Silence, a funerary tower where the Parsis or Zoroastrians left the dead bodies of their community members for excarnation as per their tradition.

The garden was officially named as the Pherozeshah Mehta Gardens in honor of Pherozeshah Mehta, a well-known lawyer, social activist, and politician. He was also known as the Lion of Bombay. In 1920, the garden was renovated and revamped to enhance its beauty and several attractions were also added over the last few decades.
